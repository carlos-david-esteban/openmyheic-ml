# How to Convert HEIC to JPG on Android (Free, No App)

If an iPhone user just sent you photos and your Android phone shows a broken image icon, a grey thumbnail, or a file that simply won't open, you've run into the classic **HEIC to JPG Android** problem. iPhones save photos as HEIC by default, and while Apple's own apps handle the format fine, plenty of Android phones, chat apps, and websites still choke on it.

The good news: you don't need to install anything. This guide covers why Android struggles with HEIC in the first place, how Samsung Gallery and Google Photos actually behave with these files, and how to **convert HEIC to JPG on Android** for free using nothing but your phone's browser.

## Why Android Can't Open HEIC Files from iPhone

HEIC (High Efficiency Image Container) is Apple's default photo format since iOS 11. It compresses images using the HEIF standard, producing files roughly half the size of an equivalent JPG at the same quality. If you want the full technical breakdown, see our guide on [what HEIC actually is](/what-is-heic).

The catch is that HEIC never became a universal standard the way JPG did. Many Android apps, older messaging platforms, and most websites still expect a `.jpg` or `.jpeg` file. When a HEIC photo lands on an Android device — via email, WhatsApp, a USB transfer, or cloud storage — anything that isn't built to decode HEIF will either refuse to open it or show a blank thumbnail.

## Does Android Support HEIC Natively?

Sort of, and this is where a lot of confusion comes from. Android has included HEIF decoding support since Android 9 (Pie), so the operating system itself *can* read the format. But actual behavior depends heavily on:

- **Android version** — older devices (Android 8 and below) have no built-in HEIF support at all.
- **Manufacturer skin** — Samsung, Google, Xiaomi, and OnePlus all handle HEIC slightly differently in their gallery and camera apps.
- **The specific app** — your gallery might open a HEIC file fine, but the same file could fail to upload to a web form, a printing service, or an older social app.

That inconsistency is why "it opened on my friend's Android but not mine" is such a common complaint.

### Samsung Gallery and HEIC Files

Samsung phones are a special case, since Samsung's own camera can also shoot in HEIF format. **Samsung HEIC to JPG** questions usually come up in one of two situations:

1. **Viewing** — Samsung Gallery can generally display HEIC photos without issue, including ones received from an iPhone.
2. **Sharing or uploading** — this is where problems start. When you try to upload a HEIC photo to a website, attach it in an app that doesn't support the format, or print it at a kiosk, it can fail silently or show a corrupted file.

Some Samsung devices offer a setting under Camera settings to save photos as JPEG instead of HEIF for new photos you take yourself — but that setting does nothing for HEIC files someone else already sent you. For those, you still need to convert them.

## Google Photos and HEIC

Google Photos handles HEIC reasonably well: it can display, back up, and organize HEIC images without complaint. The issue shows up when you try to *use* that photo somewhere else. Downloading a photo from Google Photos on some devices/browsers keeps it in HEIC format, and many upload forms, ad platforms, print services, and older apps will reject it or render it broken. If you just need to view the photo, Google Photos usually works — the conversion step becomes necessary once you need a JPG for compatibility elsewhere.
